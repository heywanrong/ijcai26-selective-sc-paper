\begin{abstract}
We study prompted chain-of-thought (CoT) with self-consistency
(SC) --- sampling $N$ traces and plurality-voting --- on
small ($<2$B) \emph{instruct} language models, the regime where
inference economics matter most and where always-SC is the
inherited default. Native RL-trained reasoners are out of scope.
We show that on this regime always-SC is strictly dominated.
Across two open instruct models (Qwen-2.5-0.5B, Gemma-3-1B) in
FP16 and MLX-Q4, on GSM8K and MATH-500, we run 8 per-task cells
and 4 task-pooled aggregates at $n{=}200$--$400$ per cell with
$1\,000{\times}$ paired-bootstrap confidence intervals. In all
$12/12$ experiments, an oracle gate that decides per-prompt whether
to invoke SC attains $109$--$174\%$ of always-SC's accuracy at
$23$--$30\%$ of its compute. The mechanism is simple: for these
models, prompted SC flips \emph{correct greedy answers to wrong}
at a rate comparable to --- and on one cell exceeding --- the rate
at which it flips wrong to right; on Qwen-Q4${\times}$MATH-500,
always-SC is $3$\,pt \emph{worse} than always-greedy. A 12-feature
logistic gate trained on a few hundred calibration prompts skips
$45$--$99\%$ of SC calls and cuts always-SC compute by $36$--$79\%$
at $\ge 98\%$ accuracy retention; a single-feature confidence gate
achieves comparable savings on the easier cells. All experiments
run on a single MacBook M4 Pro using MLX; no CUDA, no cloud
inference.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Test-time compute (TTC) has become the workhorse of modern
language-model deployment. Starting with chain-of-thought
prompting~\citep{Wei2022CoT,Kojima2022ZeroShotCoT}, a family of TTC
methods --- self-consistency sampling~\citep{Wang2023SC}, best-of-$N$
reranking with learned verifiers~\citep{Cobbe2021Verifiers,
Lightman2023LetsVerify}, tree-structured
search~\citep{Yao2023ToT}, budget-forced CoT~\citep{Snell2024TTC},
tool-integrated self-verification~\citep{Kang2025T1}, and iterative
self-refinement~\citep{Madaan2023SelfRefine} --- now underpins the
strongest open and proprietary
systems~\citep{DeepSeekR1,OpenAIO1,Chen2025LongCoTSurvey}. The
prevailing deployment recipe is simple: for every query, sample $N$
traces and plurality-vote. This \emph{always-SC} policy is the
default in every widely-used inference stack we are
aware of, and has been exhaustively validated on large
($\ge 7$B) models, where $N{=}5$ to $N{=}40$ reliably trades compute
for accuracy~\citep{Chen2025LongCoTSurvey,Snell2024TTC}.

The economics of TTC are very different for \textbf{small} ($<2$B)
\emph{instruct} language models. This is precisely the segment
where inference economics matter most: on-device deployment is
constrained by memory and battery~\citep{Wang2025MNNLLM,
Bsharat2025MobileMMLU}, cloud cost is dominated by
latency~\citep{Cao2026PER}, privacy pressures favour local
inference, and recent work has shown that distilled
1--4B language models can close a surprising fraction of the gap to
$7$B$+$ teachers on math and code~\citep{Xu2025Phi4Mini,
DeepSeekR1,Zhu2024EoTD,Wang2025EffCoT}. Yet the community has
largely inherited the always-SC recipe from large-model practice
without revisiting whether it is still the right default for
\emph{prompted} CoT on small instruct models --- as opposed to
native RL-trained reasoners, which are out of our scope. At the same time, a parallel literature on efficient TTC has
emerged: \citet{Aggarwal2023AdaptiveConsistency} and
\citet{Wan2024RASC} show that \emph{sample-count} can be cut by
dynamic stopping rules; \citet{Aghazadeh2025CGES, Kim2026ReASC}
add confidence-aware stopping; \citet{Ji2025SeerSC} predicts the
sample budget up front. These works uniformly assume that \emph{SC
at some $N{>}1$ is always worth running}. In this paper we show that
on small models this premise itself is wrong.

We revisit two questions. \textbf{(Q1) Is always-SC actually worth it
on small models?} The headline community metric is SC's accuracy
relative to greedy. But the realistic deployment baseline is not just
``greedy''; it is ``run cheap greedy first, and run SC only when the
prompt warrants''. The fair counterfactual for always-SC is an
\emph{oracle} gate that, for each prompt, picks the better of greedy
and SC. \textbf{(Q2) If always-SC is dominated, can a cheap learned
gate approximate the oracle?} If yes, \emph{selective} SC replaces
always-SC as the natural default on small models.

We answer both concretely. On Qwen-2.5-0.5B-Instruct
\citep{Qwen25Report} and Gemma-3-1B-it \citep{Gemma3Report}, each in
native FP16 and 4-bit MLX quantisation, across GSM8K
\citep{Cobbe2021Verifiers} and MATH-500 \citep{Hendrycks2021MATH}, the
oracle gate strictly dominates always-SC in all $12/12$ experiments,
with retention from $109\%$ to $174\%$. The dominance is driven by a
non-trivial \emph{harm rate}: SC flips correct greedy answers to
wrong on $3$--$10.5\%$ of prompts, comparable to (and sometimes larger
than) the rate at which it flips wrong to right. On
Qwen-Q4${\times}$MATH-500 the harm rate exceeds the help rate and
always-SC is $3$\,pt worse than always-greedy. A simple learned gate
on greedy-pass signals (a 12-feature logistic on three cells; a
single-feature confidence gate on Qwen-Q4) captures most of the
oracle's savings: $45$--$99\%$ skip-rate (i.e.\ $36$--$79\%$ of
always-SC compute removed) at $\ge 98\%$ accuracy retention on all
four pooled cells, with tight $1\,000{\times}$ bootstrap
confidence intervals (CIs) on skip-rate.

\paragraph{Relation to prior efficient-TTC work.}
Adaptive SC methods~\citep{Aggarwal2023AdaptiveConsistency,
Wan2024RASC,Aghazadeh2025CGES,Kim2026ReASC,Ji2025SeerSC,
Zhou2025RPC,Cordero2025Certified,Liu2026PETS} reduce $N$
\emph{given that SC runs}. Our gate decides whether SC runs \emph{at
all} --- the two are orthogonal, and the gate's skip decisions save
$5\times$ on skipped prompts vs.\ at most $\approx 2\times$ from
shrinking $N$ from $5$ to $2$. Cascade and router
systems~\citep{Zheng2025CITER,She2025TokenRouter,Ong2024RouteLLM,
Chen2024FrugalGPT} route \emph{between} a small and large model; our
gate is within-model, verifier-free, and requires no cloud teacher.
\citet{Chuang2024ConfidenceRoute} learn confidence tokens for
between-model routing; we instead aggregate native greedy logits
into a prompt-level score.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
(i) The first paired-bootstrap measurement of prompted SC's
``helps vs.\ hurts'' decomposition on small instruct language
models, exhibiting a
non-negligible and at one cell \emph{dominant} harm rate
(\S\ref{sec:oracle}); (ii) a counter-narrative to the ``SC is free''
assumption implicit in current TTC literature; (iii) a minimal
1--12 feature cross-validated TTC-gate that captures most of the
oracle's savings with tight skip-rate confidence intervals, alongside
an ablation showing that extra model capacity (a multi-layer
perceptron, MLP) universally hurts at this data scale
(\S\ref{sec:method}); (iv) full reproducibility on a
single MacBook M4 Pro under MLX, folded inline into the experimental
protocol.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Test-time compute.}
Chain-of-thought prompting~\citep{Wei2022CoT,Kojima2022ZeroShotCoT}
and self-consistency~\citep{Wang2023SC} established the basic
``sample-and-aggregate'' recipe. \citet{Snell2024TTC} and
\citet{Chen2024Escape} formalise its compute--accuracy scaling laws
for large models, and \citet{Chen2025LongCoTSurvey} survey the
resulting long-CoT literature. \citet{Yao2023ToT} extend it to
structured search; \citet{Lightman2023LetsVerify,
Cobbe2021Verifiers} train process- and outcome-reward verifiers for
reranking. None of this line interrogates the assumption that SC is
worth running on every prompt.

\paragraph{Adaptive self-consistency.}
Several works reduce the sample count $N$.
\citet{Aggarwal2023AdaptiveConsistency} propose count-based early
stopping on majority convergence. \citet{Wan2024RASC} weight votes by
per-sample quality. \citet{Aghazadeh2025CGES} and
\citet{Kim2026ReASC} adopt Bayesian or reliability-aware stopping
rules. \citet{Ji2025SeerSC} predict the budget up front via a
System-1 entropy estimate. \citet{Zhou2025RPC} and
\citet{Cordero2025Certified} give statistical guarantees for
sampling-based TTC; \citet{Liu2026PETS} optimises cross-prompt budget
allocation. All assume SC runs; our gate can compose orthogonally
with any of them to also decide \emph{whether} to run SC.

\paragraph{Small language models and quantisation.}
Distilled sub-2B language models~\citep{Xu2025Phi4Mini,
Zhu2024EoTD,Wang2025EffCoT,Kang2025T1,DeepSeekR1} make the
small-language-model (small-LM) ${+}$ TTC axis practically relevant.
\citet{RedHatQuant2024} report ``4-bit-for-free'' on 7B-class models
on GSM8K. \citet{Li2025QuantMath} and \citet{Li2025QuantMitigate}
systematically show that at smaller scales 4-bit post-training
quantisation (PTQ, e.g.\ AWQ~\citep{Lin2024AWQ},
GPTQ~\citep{Frantar2023GPTQ}) can induce $>10\%$ reasoning drops;
\citet{Lv2026QAT} partially recover via quantisation-aware training
(QAT).
We observe a $5$--$22$\,pt Q4-vs-FP16 drop on the two small families
we test, and show that this drop interacts adversely with SC on at
least one cell.

\paragraph{Calibration and verbalised confidence.}
Classical calibration
work~\citep{Guo2017Calibration,Kadavath2022Know} and more recent LLM
uncertainty studies~\citep{Xiong2023Uncertainty,
Chuang2024ConfidenceRoute} show that token-level confidences are a
useful, if imperfect, signal. We use only native greedy-pass logits
and simple text features; no fine-tuning, no verbaliser.

\paragraph{Routing and cascades.}
Cascade and router systems~\citep{Zheng2025CITER,She2025TokenRouter,
Ong2024RouteLLM,Chen2024FrugalGPT,Jiang2023LLMBlender} pick between
models of different sizes or providers using logit-level or learned
signals; speculative decoding~\citep{Leviathan2023Speculative}
accelerates autoregressive inference with a small draft model. Our
gate is \emph{within-model} and decides not \emph{which} model but
\emph{whether to run} TTC at all; it requires no cloud teacher, no
external verifier, and no auxiliary model.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models.}
We use Qwen-2.5-0.5B-Instruct~\citep{Qwen25Report} and
Gemma-3-1B-it~\citep{Gemma3Report}, each in two precisions:
MLX-FP16 (native bf16 cast to fp16 for MLX) and MLX-Q4 (affine
weight-only quantisation at group size~$64$). On Apple Silicon the
standard bitsandbytes-NF4 and auto-gptq stacks lack GPU kernels or
fall back to CPU at $\ge 10\times$ latency; MLX-Q4 is the practical
Apple-GPU path~\citep{MLX2024}. This cross of two model families
and two precisions yields four cells, each of which is run on both
datasets.

\paragraph{Datasets.}
We use GSM8K-1000 (the first $1\,000$ problems of the GSM8K test
split)~\citep{Cobbe2021Verifiers} and MATH-500, the HuggingFaceH4
$500$-problem evaluation subset of MATH~\citep{Hendrycks2021MATH,
Lightman2023LetsVerify}. For each of the four cells we run the
first $200$ problems per dataset, giving $1\,600$ prompt-level
observations across the per-task grid. We additionally pool the
two tasks into a $400$-per-cell aggregate, to test the gate's
task-agnosticism.

\paragraph{Prompt and sampling protocol.}
One in-context exemplar is followed by the zero-shot CoT
cue~\citep{Kojima2022ZeroShotCoT} ``Let's think step by step.''
Decoding uses temperature $0.7$ and top-$p$ $0.95$ for sampled
traces, and $T{=}0$ (greedy) for the deterministic pass. Per prompt
we collect one greedy answer and $K{=}5$ sampled answers; SC@$5$ is
plurality voting over the $K$ samples, with sum-log-probability
tiebreak. Answer parsing is a layered regex over \verb|\boxed{}|,
``The answer is $X$'', ``\verb|####|'', and finally the last numeric
expression in the output. All runs use seed~$1$ and deterministic
MLX sampling.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
Raw accuracies are reported with Wilson $95\%$ confidence intervals
(CIs). FP16-vs-Q4 deltas use paired $1\,000{\times}$ bootstrap
(paired at the prompt level). Gate operating points at fixed
threshold $\tau$ use per-cell $1\,000{\times}$ bootstrap over prompts. Where we report
\emph{retention} $R(\tau) = \text{acc}_\text{gate}(\tau) /
\text{acc}_\text{always-SC}$, the denominator is the empirical SC@$5$
accuracy on the same cell.

\paragraph{Hardware and compute budget.}
All experiments run on a single MacBook Pro M4 Pro ($24$\,GB unified
memory, $16$-core Apple M4 GPU, MLX 0.31 and \texttt{mlx-lm} 0.31
plus PyTorch 2.11 with Metal Performance Shaders (MPS), Python
3.13); no CUDA and no cloud API.
The main generation pass (four cells $\times$ $200$ prompts
$\times$ six generations per prompt) takes $\sim 3$\,h on GSM8K and
$\sim 4.5$\,h on MATH-500. Consolidation (bootstrap resampling, featurisation, gate
fitting, plotting) completes in under three minutes. Total
GPU-hours: $\approx 7.5$. Released code, cached generation records,
and seeds reproduce every table and figure end-to-end.

\section{The Oracle-Dominance Finding}
\label{sec:oracle}

\subsection{Structural decomposition}

For any prompt $x\in\mathcal{X}$, let
$g(x),s(x)\in\{0,1\}$ denote the indicators that greedy and SC@$5$
are correct, respectively. Define the \emph{help} and \emph{harm}
indicators
\begin{align*}
y(x) &\;=\; \mathbf{1}[s(x){=}1 \wedge g(x){=}0],\\
h(x) &\;=\; \mathbf{1}[g(x){=}1 \wedge s(x){=}0].
\end{align*}
Let $\pi$ denote the test distribution; taking
$\mathbb{E}_\pi[\cdot]$ gives
\begin{align}
\alpha_\mathrm{g}   &\;=\; \mathbb{E}[g],\\
\alpha_\mathrm{sc}  &\;=\; \mathbb{E}[s] \;=\; \mathbb{E}[g] + \mathbb{E}[y] - \mathbb{E}[h],\\
\alpha_\mathrm{o}   &\;=\; \mathbb{E}[\max(g,s)] \;=\; \mathbb{E}[g] + \mathbb{E}[y].
\end{align}
The oracle's gain over always-SC is therefore exactly
\begin{equation}
\alpha_\mathrm{o} - \alpha_\mathrm{sc} \;=\; \mathbb{E}[h],
\label{eq:oracle-gap}
\end{equation}
and \emph{always-SC is strictly dominated whenever} $\mathbb{E}[h]>0$.
The community narrative around SC implicitly assumes
$\mathbb{E}[h]\ll\mathbb{E}[y]$, which holds for large models but, as
we show below, does not hold for small ones.

\subsection{Empirical measurement}

Figure~\ref{fig:oracle} plots the empirical oracle-retention ratio
$\rho=\alpha_\mathrm{o}/\alpha_\mathrm{sc}$ across all $12$
experiments ($4$ model-precision cells $\times$ ($2$ datasets
$+$ pooled)). Every bar is at or above the $100\%$ always-SC
reference (dashed line), and the outlier at
$174\%$ corresponds to Qwen-0.5B-Q4 on MATH-500, where always-SC
leaves $7$\,pt of accessible accuracy on the table --- a
$74$\,pt oracle margin over always-SC. The remaining cells retain
between $109\%$ (Gemma-FP16 on GSM8K) and $146\%$ (Qwen-Q4 pooled).
The dominance pattern is invariant to dataset, model family, and
precision: not a single cell places the oracle at or below
always-SC. In particular, pooled retention ($110\%$--$146\%$, with
$125\%$--$146\%$ on three of four pooled cells and $110\%$ on the
easiest cell Gemma-FP16) tracks per-task retention, which is what we
would hope from a gate whose training transfers across tasks.

\subsection{When does SC hurt?}

The harm event $h$ requires a wrong answer to \emph{win a plurality
vote} over the correct one in five samples. This happens when (i)
the model's distribution over candidate answers is diffuse, and
(ii) the wrong-majority mode is entropically cheap under the
sampler. On small models both conditions hold on a measurable
fraction of hard prompts. On Qwen-Q4${\times}$MATH, post-quantisation
miscalibration widens the candidate-answer distribution further, so
the harm rate exceeds the help rate. SC's strength on large models
is that $\mathbb{E}[h]\ll\mathbb{E}[y]$ --- not that
$\mathbb{E}[h]\approx 0$; on small models both rates are small and
comparable, and plurality voting cannot distinguish the two cases.

\section{The TTC-Gate}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Problem formulation}

We want a scalar score function
\(
\sigma : \mathcal{X} \to [0,1]
\)
that maps each prompt, given its greedy-pass statistics, to a
``run-SC'' propensity. For threshold $\tau\in[0,1]$, the
gate policy is
\begin{equation}
\pi_\tau(x) \;=\;
\begin{cases}
\text{accept SC@}5 \text{ (cost } 5\text{)} & \text{if } \sigma(x)\ge\tau,\\
\text{accept greedy (cost } 1\text{)} & \text{if } \sigma(x)<\tau.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:policy}
\end{equation}
Let $a_\pi(x)\in\{0,1\}$ denote the policy's correctness and
$c_\pi(x)\in\{1,5\}$ its cost. The two deployment quantities of
interest are the \emph{cost ratio} $C(\tau) =
\mathbb{E}_\pi[c_\pi]/1$ and the \emph{accuracy retention} $R(\tau)
= \mathbb{E}_\pi[a_\pi]/\alpha_\mathrm{sc}$. The goal is to
maximise skip rate $s(\tau)=\Pr[\sigma(x)<\tau]$ (equivalently,
minimise $C(\tau)$) subject to $R(\tau)\ge R_0$.

\subsection{Gate instances}

We instantiate three gates, ordered by model capacity:

\paragraph{\textsc{conf\_only}.}
A single hand-crafted feature: the mean greedy-pass token margin
confidence
\(
\mathrm{mc}(x) = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}
\big(p_t^{(1)} - p_t^{(2)}\big),
\)
where $p_t^{(1)},p_t^{(2)}$ are the top-1 and top-2 softmax
probabilities at greedy-step $t$. The gate score is
$\sigma_{\text{conf}}(x) = 1 - \mathrm{mc}(x)$; no learning, $\tau$
is the only parameter.

\paragraph{\textsc{logistic}.}
A 12-feature $L_2$-regularised class-balanced logistic regression.
The feature vector $\phi(x)\in\mathbb{R}^{12}$ concatenates four
greedy-pass statistics --- mean margin confidence $\mathrm{mc}(x)$,
mean top-$5$ entropy, mean full-vocab entropy, and greedy token count
--- and eight prompt-text statistics --- length in characters, digit
count, arithmetic-operator count, comma count, newline count,
step-marker count (``Step'', ``\#''), and two redundant length
features. Writing $\tilde\phi(x)=\Sigma^{-1/2}(\phi(x)-\mu)$ for the
train-set standardisation, the gate is
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\text{log}}(x) \;=\; \sigma\!\big(w^\top\tilde\phi(x)+b\big),\;\;
\sigma(u)=\tfrac{1}{1+e^{-u}},
\label{eq:logistic}
\end{equation}
fitted by minimising
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}(w,b) \;=\;
&-\sum_{i=1}^{n} \omega_{y_i}\log\Pr\!\big(y_i\mid\tilde\phi(x_i);w,b\big)\notag\\
&+ \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|w\|_2^2,
\end{align}
with training label $y_i=h(x_i)\vee y(x_i)$ interpreted as ``SC
changes the greedy verdict'' and class weights
$\omega_{y_i}\!\propto\!1/\Pr(y=y_i)$ to counter class imbalance
($\lambda{=}1$, scikit-learn defaults otherwise).

\paragraph{\textsc{mlp}.}
A two-hidden-layer MLP with widths $32\times 16$ over the same
$\tilde\phi(x)$, ReLU activations, $L_2$ regularisation on all
weights, trained with the same class-balanced cross-entropy as
\textsc{logistic}.

\subsection{Training and threshold selection}

All gates are fit via $5$-fold cross-validation within each cell,
giving out-of-fold scores $\{\sigma(x_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ that are never
used to pick their own threshold. The operating threshold is
\begin{equation}
\tau^{\star} \;=\;
\arg\max_{\tau\in[0,1]}\; s(\tau)
\quad\text{s.t.}\quad
R(\tau)\ge R_0,
\label{eq:tau}
\end{equation}
with $R_0=0.98$. We then fix $\tau^{\star}$ and bootstrap-resample
prompts $1\,000$ times to obtain $95\%$ CIs for the operating-point
triple $(s,C,R)$. The threshold-selection objective in
Eq.~\ref{eq:tau} picks the \emph{maximum compute saving} compatible
with near-lossless accuracy, which is the regime small-model TTC
deployments actually care about.

\subsection{Feature and capacity ablation}
\label{sec:ablation}

We compare \textsc{conf\_only} ($1$ feature), \textsc{logistic}
($12$ features) and \textsc{mlp} ($12$ features, $32\times 16$
hidden) at both the $n{=}200$ per-task scale and the $n{=}400$
pooled scale. Three consistent trends emerge.

At $n{=}200$ per cell, \textsc{conf\_only} is frequently the best
gate, \textsc{logistic} is competitive, and \textsc{mlp} often
collapses to $\tau{=}0$ (i.e.\ ``always run SC'') because the
class-balanced training signal is thin: $160$ training prompts with
a base rate of roughly $8$--$23\%$ positive labels (i.e.\ $13$--$36$
positives per fold) give the MLP too little information to beat a
linear model. At $n{=}400$ pooled,
\textsc{logistic} overtakes \textsc{conf\_only} on $3/4$ cells: with
more data the weighted combination of $12$ features beats the
single strongest one. And in no cell, at any scale, does
\textsc{mlp} win: the decision surface is essentially linear in
scaled features, so extra capacity over-fits the training folds.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why does always-SC fail on small models?}
Eq.~\ref{eq:oracle-gap} gives the closed-form answer:
$\alpha_\mathrm{o}-\alpha_\mathrm{sc}=\mathbb{E}[h]$, and on small
models $\mathbb{E}[h]$ is structurally non-negligible. The
hardness on small models is concentrated on prompts where the
model's candidate-answer distribution is diffuse, and plurality
voting can elevate a wrong mode above a correct one precisely when
the correct mode is a thin plurality rather than a strict majority.
This is consistent with theoretical findings that SC's statistical
guarantees require mild concentration around the correct
mode~\citep{Cordero2025Certified,Zhou2025RPC}; at small scale that
concentration is often absent. It also explains why efficient-SC
methods that \emph{shrink $N$}~\citep{Aggarwal2023AdaptiveConsistency,
Aghazadeh2025CGES,Kim2026ReASC} cannot fix the problem: they reduce
the variance of the estimate but not its bias.

\paragraph{Why does quantisation amplify the failure?}
On Qwen-Q4${\times}$MATH-500, 4-bit quantisation drops greedy
accuracy by $5.5$\,pt relative to FP16 (Table~\ref{tab:q4}). It
also re-shapes the sampling distribution: we observe the help rate
falling by $2$\,pt and the harm rate rising by $2.5$\,pt, which
inverts the sign of $\Delta_\mathrm{sc}$. This is the same
phenomenon flagged by \citet{Li2025QuantMath,Li2025QuantMitigate}:
post-training quantisation disproportionately harms the earliest
reasoning steps, and those early errors cascade through a multi-
sample vote.

\paragraph{Why is Gemma's gate weaker than Qwen's?}
Across the six Qwen cells (two datasets, both precisions, plus
pooled), the best gate skips $59$--$100\%$ of prompts, while across
the six Gemma cells the best gate skips only $10$--$58\%$. Two
hypotheses. First, Gemma-3's reinforcement learning from human
feedback (RLHF) pipeline~\citep{Gemma3Report} may compress
greedy-pass confidence into a narrower range, reducing
feature informativeness. Second, Gemma-3's sliding-window attention
may interact with per-token margins differently from Qwen's dense
attention, changing the mapping from uncertainty to SC-worthiness.
A controlled follow-up on the same family across precisions is a
natural extension.

\paragraph{Why do deeper MLPs lose?}
Per-cell $n{=}160$ training prompts with $12$ features and a
$8$--$23\%$ positive base rate (label $h\vee y$) is firmly in
linear-model territory.
Extra nonlinearity is over-parameterisation; this is the well-known
small-data corner of the bias--variance trade-off. On
pooled $n{=}400$ the MLP becomes competitive but still does not
win, consistent with the visibly linear coefficient structure in
Table~\ref{tab:coef}.

\paragraph{What the gate's coefficients encode.}
Beyond entropy and margin confidence, two text features --- operator
and digit counts --- survive $L_2$ regularisation with positive
weight (Table~\ref{tab:coef}). Once the model has reported its own
uncertainty, the prompt still adds residual signal that ``more
arithmetic $\to$ more benefit from multiple samples'', echoing the
surface-form/trace-variance link of
\citet{Lin2024SCAnalysis}, and explaining why \textsc{conf\_only}
loses to \textsc{logistic} on three of four pooled cells.

\paragraph{Calibration-data scale, not model capacity, is the binding constraint.}
Pooling to $n{=}400$ unblocks a recipe that $n{=}200$ per-task data
cannot: on Gemma-Q4, \textsc{logistic} climbs from $19.6\%$
per-task to $57.9\%$ pooled skip-rate; on Qwen-FP16, from $66.1\%$
to $86.0\%$. The deployment rule is concrete --- with $\sim\!200$
labelled prompts a one-feature confidence gate is the safer
default; with $\sim\!400$ the 12-feature linear gate is uniformly
preferred, and extra capacity is still wasted.

\paragraph{Composition and deployment.}
Adaptive-$N$ methods~\citep{Aggarwal2023AdaptiveConsistency,
Wan2024RASC,Manvi2024AdaptiveSampling} reduce $N$ \emph{given that
SC runs}; our gate decides whether it runs, so the two axes
multiply. The gate itself is drop-in: no base-model retraining, no
verifier, no sampler change, and a single scalar threshold
$\tau^{\star}$ that an operator re-tunes per service tier from the
$(C,R)$ Pareto curve at zero extra inference cost. For on-device
reasoning where latency and energy
dominate~\citep{Wang2025MNNLLM,Bsharat2025MobileMMLU,Cao2026PER},
removing $36$--$79\%$ of always-SC compute maps directly to
user-visible latency, and the gate composes \emph{before} any
cross-model cascade~\citep{Zheng2025CITER,Ong2024RouteLLM,
Chen2024FrugalGPT}.

\section*{Limitations}

Five caveats qualify the scope of our findings. First, we ran
each cell with a single random seed. Pooled $n{=}400$ tightens
skip-rate CIs to $\pm 3$\,pt but retention CIs remain wide
because the denominator $\alpha_\mathrm{sc}$ is itself small;
three seeds would tighten retention further. Second, both families
are general-purpose instruct models, whose noisy prompt-induced CoT
likely drives the high harm rate; RL-distilled reasoners
(e.g.\ DeepSeek-R1-Distill-1.5B~\citep{DeepSeekR1}) should
concentrate the correct mode and shrink $\mathbb{E}[h]$, which we
did not test. Generalisation to coding, commonsense, or $3$\,B$+$ models is
also untested. Third, we fix $N{=}5$. At larger
$N$ the harm rate $\mathbb{E}[h]$ should fall (and the gate's
threshold will need re-tuning), but the qualitative claim --- that
$\mathbb{E}[h]$ is non-negligible --- should remain. Fourth, our
gate uses a single fixed threshold $\tau^{\star}$; a
difficulty-bucketed adaptive $\tau$ is an obvious extension.
Finally, we do not study a cheap post-SC verifier; with one, the
right analysis is a three-way gate (greedy / SC / SC + verifier),
which composes cleanly with our two-way gate.

\section{Conclusion}

For prompted CoT + SC on small ($<2$B) instruct language models,
the default ``always run SC'' recipe is strictly dominated by a
per-prompt oracle gate in every
one of our $12$ experiments. The dominance is closed-form: the
oracle's margin over always-SC equals the rate at which SC flips
correct greedy answers to wrong, and on small models that rate is
structurally non-negligible. A 12-feature logistic gate trained
via 5-fold cross-validation on a few hundred calibration prompts
captures most of the oracle's savings: it skips $45$--$99\%$ of
SC calls and removes $36$--$79\%$ of always-SC compute at
$\ge 98\%$ accuracy retention in the pooled $n{=}400$ analysis,
with tight bootstrap CIs. The gate is
model-local, verifier-free, runs on-device in under a minute of
consolidation time on a single MacBook, and is deploy-ready.
Before reporting ``SC@$N$ improves accuracy'' on small models,
check the harm rate.
